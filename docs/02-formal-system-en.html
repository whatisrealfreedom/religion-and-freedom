2. What is an Axiomatic Formal System? ⚙️✨

Like a mighty tree that builds countless branches of laws and beauty from a few strong roots — but this time for human freedom

This section is crucial because it contains the heart of Jannatkhah's theory. Let's proceed calmly, step by step, like a story, starting from the simplest level to reach the depth. Even if you have no background in philosophy or mathematics, everything will become clear to you by the end of this page.

First: What Exactly is an Axiomatic Formal System? (Explanation for Everyone)

Imagine you want to create a game, like chess. For the game to be fair and stable, you need a few fundamental rules that everyone accepts and no one can change. These fundamental rules are called axioms (postulates — things we accept without proof because they are self-evident).

Now an axiomatic formal system means an organized and precise structure that:

  • Starts with a limited number of axioms
  • Builds thousands of new laws from those axioms using simple logical rules (like addition and multiplication in mathematics)
  • Always remains stable, has no contradictions, and does not change over time

This is like building a solid building: you place a few main pillars (axioms), then with engineering laws, you raise the entire structure.

Simple Everyday Example: School Geometry (Euclidean)

Remember studying geometry in school? All geometric laws (like the area of a triangle, the Pythagorean theorem) were built from just 5 fundamental principles (axioms). Let's look at two of them:

  • Axiom 1: Only one straight line passes through two points. (Simple and self-evident, right?)
  • Famous Axiom 5 (Parallel Lines): If a straight line intersects two other lines and determines the angles, parallel lines never meet.

From these few simple principles, thousands of theorems are built: the area of a circle, the volume of a cube, even the design of bridges and buildings!

If one of these axioms changes, all of geometry changes — but as long as they remain constant, the system is stable.

More Advanced Example: Natural Numbers (How All Mathematics is Built)

Now let's think about numbers. How can we prove that 2+2=4? From more basic principles!

Peano's Axioms (the simplest foundation of mathematics):

  • 0 is a natural number.
  • Every natural number has a "successor" (like 0 → 1, 1 → 2, etc.).
  • No two numbers have the same successor.
  • If something is true for 0 and all successors, it is true for all numbers (principle of induction).

From these few principles, all addition, multiplication, exponentiation, and even advanced mathematics is built!

These systems show that with a few solid principles, you can build a complete world without contradictions.
